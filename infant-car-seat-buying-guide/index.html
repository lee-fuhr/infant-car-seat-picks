How to choose infant car seats

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

To choose an infant car seat, focus on safety certifications, ease of installation, and a proper fit for your vehicle and baby. Look for a seat with a five-point harness, side-impact protection, and a base that can be installed tightly with either seat belt or LATCH. Then test the fit in your car and consider your budget and how long you plan to use a rear-facing-only seat before moving to a convertible.

What are the main types of infant car seats?

Infant car seats are rear-facing only seats designed for newborns and smaller babies. They come as a bucket-style seat with a separate base that stays in the car, and a handle for carrying the baby outside the vehicle. Most have a maximum weight limit around 30 to 35 pounds and a height limit that allows most babies up to about 32 inches. Convertible car seats, by contrast, can be used rear-facing first and then forward-facing later, but they are not portable and stay in the car. If you want the convenience of lifting your sleeping baby out without unbuckling, an infant seat is the better choice for the first year. Many families buy an infant seat first and then switch to a convertible around age one or when the baby outgrows the infant seat.

Which safety features actually matter in an infant car seat?

Every car seat sold in the U.S. must meet federal safety standards, so all seats are safe when used correctly. The most important feature is a five-point harness that goes over both shoulders, both hips, and between the legs. Look for harness straps that adjust from a front adjuster so you can tighten them without rethreading. Side-impact protection is also worth prioritizing. Many seats have extra foam or energy-absorbing shells in the headrest area. Also check that the seat has a recline adjuster or built-in level indicator to help you get the correct angle for a newborn, who needs a more reclined position to keep their airway open.

How do I know if a car seat will fit my car?

Not all car seats fit all vehicles well, especially in compact cars or cars with sloped seats. The best approach is to try the seat in your car before buying, or at least check the manufacturer’s vehicle compatibility list. Look for the LATCH anchors in your back seat, and check how much front-seat legroom you need to sacrifice to get the seat installed at the correct angle. Pay attention to the length of the base. Some bases are very long and can push the front passenger seat far forward. Also check that your seat belt or LATCH connectors are easy to access and that you can tighten the seat to the point where it moves less than one inch side-to-side at the belt path. If you have a 3-across seating situation, measure the width of the infant seat base – many are fairly wide.

What should I look for in ease of installation?

An easy-to-install seat saves frustration and reduces the chance of misuse. Features that help include a bubble level or angle indicator built into the base, a push-on LATCH connector that clicks without needing to pull a strap, and a lock-off mechanism that holds the seat belt tight. Seats with an adjustable recline foot also help get the right angle on different vehicle seats. Read owner feedback about how hard it is to get a tight install. Some seats are notorious for requiring extra effort, such as needing to push down hard on the base while tightening. Also consider whether the base has a tension indicator that turns green when tight enough – that feature is now common on many newer models.

How much should I expect to spend on a good infant car seat?

Infant car seat prices vary widely, from very budget-friendly options under a few hundred dollars to premium models that cost twice that or more. Spending more often gets you added convenience features like a no-rethread harness, an adjustable recline, higher weight limits, and more plush padding. But a budget-friendly seat that is certified and installed correctly is just as safe as an expensive one. Consider that you will also need to buy a second base for a second car, and possibly a travel system stroller if the seat is compatible. The total cost can add up, so decide what features matter most to you – for example, a lightweight seat for carrying up stairs or a mode with a simple click-in base might be worth extra.

When should I switch from an infant seat to a convertible seat?

Switch when your baby reaches the maximum weight or height limit for the infant seat, whichever comes first. Most babies outgrow infant seats by height before weight, especially long babies. If the top of your baby’s head is less than an inch below the top of the shell, it’s time to move up. Also consider switching if the seat is becoming a struggle to carry (infant seats get heavy as babies grow) or if you want to keep your child rear-facing longer. Convertible seats typically allow rear-facing to 40 pounds or more, which is safer for young toddlers. Many parents switch between 9 and 15 months, but keeping your child rear-facing to age two or beyond is the safest practice.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use an infant car seat that was in a minor crash?

Generally no – after any moderate or severe crash, replace the seat. For minor crashes (low speed, no visible damage, airbags did not deploy), some manufacturers allow continued use, but most recommend replacing it to be safe. Always check the manufacturer’s crash replacement policy.

Do I need to buy a new base if I switch the seat between cars?

Yes, you can buy an extra base for each vehicle to avoid reinstalling the seat each time. However, many infant seats can also be installed without the base using the vehicle seat belt – check the manual for the correct procedure. An extra base makes swapping quick and reduces installation errors.

What is the difference between LATCH and seat belt installation?

LATCH uses lower anchors and a top tether (for forward-facing) built into the vehicle seat, while seat belt installation uses the vehicle’s lap/shoulder belt. Both are equally safe if done correctly. LATCH has weight limits (usually 65 pounds combined child and seat weight), so for heavier children you must use the seat belt.

How long can my baby stay in an infant car seat outside the car?

Infant car seats are designed for travel, not for sleeping. The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends limiting continuous time in the seat to 30 minutes to an hour for newborns, and no prolonged use for any baby. Never use the seat as a substitute for a crib or bassinet.

Should I buy an infant car seat that has a stroller travel system?

A travel system is convenient if you plan to transition a sleeping baby from car to stroller without waking them. Many major brands offer compatible strollers that click directly into the seat base. Make sure the stroller is easy to fold and maneuver and fits your lifestyle before buying the combo.

Are heavier infant car seats safer than lighter ones?

No – weight does not correlate with safety. Lighter seats are easier to carry and install, which actually reduces the risk of misuse. A seat’s safety depends on its design, materials, and how well it is installed, not on its total weight.

In shortChoosing the right infant car seat comes down to finding one that fits your car, your budget, and your daily routine. Prioritize safety features like a five-point harness and side-impact protection, but don’t overlook installation ease – a seat you can install correctly every time is the safest seat for your family. Take time to test the fit in your vehicle and read reliable owner reviews before buying. A well-chosen infant seat will give you peace of mind and a convenient way to travel with your baby during those first busy months.
